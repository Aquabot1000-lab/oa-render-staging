**Comment:**

Really thorough explanation of Georgia's appeal process. The 45-day window is something every Georgia homeowner needs to have on their calendar — it goes by fast, especially if you receive your notice during a busy time of year.

A few additional points for Georgia homeowners:

The PT-311A form is straightforward but make sure you check the box for "value" appeal (not just "uniformity" unless you have specific evidence of unequal treatment). Most residential appeals succeed on the basis of comparable sales showing a lower market value.

Also, Georgia's Board of Equalization hearing process can vary significantly by county. In metro Atlanta counties like Fulton and DeKalb, hearings are more formal and the boards see hundreds of cases. In smaller counties, the process is more conversational. Adjust your presentation style accordingly.

One thing the article doesn't mention: if you disagree with the BOE decision, you can appeal to Superior Court or choose binding arbitration (for properties valued under $500,000). The arbitration option is underutilized and can be very effective.

## Blog #8 — Washington State

**Blog URL:** https://www.connectingyouhome.com/blog/how-to-appeal-your-property-tax-valuation-in-king-or-snohomish-county-2025-homeowners-guide

**Blog Title:** How to Appeal Your Property Tax Valuation in King or Snohomish County (2025 Homeowners Guide)

**Comment:**

This is a really helpful guide for King and Snohomish County homeowners. The eAppeals system in King County is surprisingly user-friendly once you know it exists — many homeowners don't realize they can file entirely online.

One thing I'd emphasize: Washington State law presumes the assessor is correct (RCW 84.40.0301), which means the burden of proof is on the homeowner. This makes your evidence quality even more important than in states like Texas where the burden is lower. You need strong comparable sales — ideally 3+ sold properties within the last year, similar in size and location, that sold for less than your assessed value.

Also, for homeowners who are unsuccessful at the County Board of Equalization, don't give up. You can appeal to the Washington State Board of Tax Appeals (WSBTA), which provides a fresh review. Just note that if you took the informal appeal route at the county level, you may not be able to go to Superior Court afterward — the formal hearing preserves more appeal rights.

Washington's property tax system is different from Texas and Georgia, but the fundamental principle is the same: if your assessed value exceeds fair market value, you have the right to challenge it. Our team at overassessed.ai works with Washington State homeowners on a contingency basis and we're familiar with the specific county board processes across WA.
